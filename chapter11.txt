

Some silences aren’t empty.
They’re filled with things we don’t know how to say.

The days after that call felt normal on the outside. Classes continued. People laughed. Assignments piled up. Life didn’t pause just because something inside me had.

But I noticed the difference.

Not in her absence.
In the way her presence had changed.

We still spoke. Still shared small things. Still stayed connected through routine. But there was a layer now — thin, invisible, but real.

Like both of us were careful not to step into something unnamed.


---

It was Monday morning when I saw her in the corridor.

She was standing near the notice board, reading something, her bag hanging loosely from one shoulder. People moved around her, voices blending into the usual chaos of college mornings.

I slowed down without meaning to.

She turned, noticed me, and smiled.

Not wide. Not forced. Just natural.

“Morning,” she said.

“Morning.”

We stood there for a second longer than necessary.

Then she stepped aside, letting others pass.

“You saw the timetable change?” she asked.

“Yeah.”

“It’s worse.”

I laughed softly. “It’s always worse.”

She smiled again, but her eyes stayed thoughtful, like she was holding something back.

We walked together toward class. Not close. Not distant. Just side by side.

And that’s when I realised — this was new.

Earlier, we filled space with words. Now we shared it with silence.


---

Inside class, Karti was already there, leaning back in his chair like he owned the room.

He saw us walk in together and raised an eyebrow.

“Oh ho.”

I ignored him.

He didn’t ignore me.

As soon as she moved to her seat, he leaned closer and whispered, “You’re both weird these days.”

“How?”

“Like you’re talking less, but thinking more.”

“That makes no sense.”

“It makes perfect sense,” he said, smirking. “You just don’t want it to.”

I rolled my eyes, but his words stayed.

Because they were true.


---

That evening, I found myself rereading old chats.

Not intentionally. Just scrolling.

Messages that once felt ordinary now carried weight.

Random jokes. Late-night thoughts. Half-finished conversations.

All of it felt… closer.

Like evidence of something slowly growing.

And that scared me.

Because growth means attachment.

And attachment means risk.


---

A few days later, we were in the library.

She sat across from me, flipping through notes, occasionally underlining something with careful focus.

The sunlight coming through the window fell across her notebook, then her hands, then her face.

She looked up suddenly.

“You’re staring.”

I blinked. “I wasn’t.”

“You were.”

“Okay… maybe a little.”

She smiled, shaking her head slightly, then went back to reading.

But after a moment, she spoke again.

“You overthink a lot, don’t you?”

I leaned back in my chair. “Recently? Yeah.”

“About?”

“Life. Studies. Random things.”

She looked at me for a second, like she was deciding whether to ask more.

Then quietly, “Not just that.”

I didn’t respond.

Because if she asked one question deeper, I didn’t know what I’d say.


---

That night, Karti and I were sitting on the hostel steps.

The corridor behind us buzzed with noise — someone playing music, someone arguing, someone laughing too loudly.

He nudged me suddenly.

“You’ve changed.”

“You keep saying that.”

“Because it’s true.”

“How?”

“You pause before replying now. Earlier you never did.”

I didn’t answer.

He continued, “And when your phone lights up, your entire face changes. Then you try to act normal. Which makes it worse.”

“Shut up.”

He laughed.

“I’m serious. You care.”

I looked away.

“Maybe.”

He didn’t tease after that.

Just sat there quietly beside me.

And for the first time, I realised — people around me were noticing what I was still trying to understand.


---

The next day, Aadhya’s message came late at night.

Aadhya: “You awake?”

Me: “Yeah.”

Aadhya: “Can I ask something?”

Me: “Hmm.”

A pause.

Then—

Aadhya: “Have you ever felt close to someone without knowing when it happened?”

I stared at the screen.

My heart beat slower.

Me: “Yeah.”

Aadhya: “Scary, right?”

I exhaled slowly.

Me: “Yeah.”

Typing dots appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

Then—

Aadhya: “Because you don’t know what to do next.”

I didn’t reply immediately.

Because I didn’t know either.

And for the first time, I realised something important.

This wasn’t just happening inside me.

She felt it too.

Maybe not the same way.
Maybe not with the same weight.

But she felt the shift.

And that changed everything.


---

That night, sleep didn’t come easily.

Not because of fear.

Because of clarity.

For weeks, I had been trying to ignore it, downplay it, keep it undefined.

But now…

It had a shape.

Not love.
Not yet.

But something that was slowly moving toward it.

Something fragile.

Something real.

And the scariest part?

It wasn’t just mine anymore.

It was ours.
