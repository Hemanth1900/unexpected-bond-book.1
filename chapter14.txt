
Group study never actually meant studying.

It meant gathering in one place, opening books for formality, and then talking about everything except what needed to be done.

That afternoon, we had taken over one corner of the library’s discussion room. Karti was sprawled across a chair like he owned it. Bhagya was flipping through a reference book with surprising focus. Two others from class argued over formulas they barely understood.

And Aadhya sat across from me.

Notebook open. Pen in hand. Pretending to read.

I was doing the same.




“Okay listen,” Karti said suddenly, slapping his notebook shut. “If we fail this internal, I’m blaming the education system.”

“You were blaming the weather yesterday,” Bhagya replied without looking up.

“Both are responsible.”

“Your laziness is responsible,” I said.

He gasped dramatically. “Betrayal.”

Aadhya laughed softly.

Not loud. Just enough to shift the air.

I noticed.




We tried studying for a while.

Bhagya asked doubts. Karti complained about calculations. Someone went out to bring chai and returned without change. The room filled with scattered conversations and occasional bursts of laughter.

Normal chaos.

But every now and then, my attention drifted back to Aadhya.

The way she tapped her pen when thinking.
The way she read lines twice before underlining.
The way she paused before speaking, as if choosing words carefully.

Small things.

But they stayed.




“Charan,” she said, breaking my focus.

“Hmm?”

“You solved question three?”

“Yeah.”

She slid her notebook toward me. “Explain?”

I leaned forward, pointing at the steps.

“This part… you skipped a unit conversion.”

She watched closely.

Then frowned slightly.

“Wait… then this entire value changes?”

“Yeah.”

She sighed, leaning back. “Great. I just wasted twenty minutes.”

“Character development.”

She gave me a look. “Very funny.”

I smiled.

She didn’t look away immediately.




“Bro,” Karti interrupted loudly, leaning over. “Explain to me also. I refuse to fail alone.”

“You refuse to pass alone too,” Bhagya added.

“Team spirit.”

“Excuse.”

Laughter broke out again.

The tension dissolved.




After a while, conversations split.

Bhagya moved to another table with notes. Two others left to photocopy material. Karti stayed back, but was more invested in his phone than the book.

It left me and Aadhya at the same table.

Closer now.

Quieter.




“You actually like studying, don’t you?” she asked.

“Not really.”

“You look serious.”

“That’s survival mode.”

She smiled. “Fair.”

A pause.

Then—

“You don’t talk much in groups,” she said.

“I talk when needed.”

“And when not needed?”

“I listen.”

She tilted her head slightly. “To what?”

“Everything.”

She looked thoughtful.

“That explains why you notice things others don’t.”

I shrugged.

“You do too,” I said.

She didn’t deny it.




Karti suddenly looked up from his phone.

“You two look like you’re having a serious life discussion. Should I leave?”

“Please do,” Aadhya said instantly.

He clutched his chest dramatically. “Pain.”

Bhagya, from the other table, called out, “Go. Let them study in peace.”

Karti looked between all of us, then leaned toward me and whispered loudly enough for everyone to hear:

“Careful bro. Group study turning into private tuition.”

I rolled my eyes.

Aadhya shook her head, hiding a smile.




“Ignore him,” I said.

“I am,” she replied.

But her expression had changed slightly.

Not uncomfortable.

Just aware.



We tried studying again.

But focus was gone.

Instead, we ended up talking.

About professors.

About subjects we liked.

About how this semester felt heavier than expected.

Normal conversations.

But stretched longer.

More personal.




“You ever regret choosing this course?” she asked.

“Sometimes.”

“Why?”

“Feels like everyone else knows what they’re doing.”

“And you don’t?”

“I’m figuring it out as I go.”

She nodded slowly.

“That’s most people.”

“Not you?”

“I look confident,” she said. “That doesn’t mean I am.”

That surprised me.

“You don’t seem unsure.”

“I am. I just don’t show it.”

I laughed softly. “Same.”

For a second, we just looked at each other.

Recognition.




“Okay enough,” Karti announced suddenly, standing up. “My brain has stopped working.”

“Did it ever start?” Bhagya asked.

“Rude.”

“Accurate.”

He grabbed his bag and stretched. “I’m going to the canteen. Anyone coming?”

Everyone declined.

He left dramatically anyway.




Silence settled again.

Just the hum of the library AC and occasional page turns.

Aadhya rested her chin lightly on her hand, looking at the notebook.

“You know,” she said quietly, “group study usually feels exhausting.”

“And today?”

“Not really.”

I waited.

She glanced at me briefly.

“Feels… easy.”

The word stayed.

I didn’t respond.

Because I felt the same.



After a while, she closed her notebook.

“I should go.”

“Yeah.”

We packed slowly.

Walked out of the library together.

The corridor was quieter now, late afternoon sunlight filtering through windows.

At the staircase, she stopped.

“Thanks,” she said.

“For?”

“For making studying less painful.”

“Anytime.”

She smiled, then started walking down.

Halfway, she turned back.

“And Charan?”

“Hmm?”

“You overthink less when you talk.”

I exhaled softly.

“I’ll try.”

She nodded once.

Then left.




Standing there, I realised something.

This wasn’t just comfort anymore.

It was rhythm.

We were starting to understand each other without explaining everything.

And that…

was new.

And dangerous.

Because when someone becomes part of your routine…

you stop preparing for their absence.

And that’s when losing them starts to matter.
