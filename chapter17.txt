


The campus didn’t feel like itself that evening.

Corridors that usually echoed with footsteps were filled with laughter. Lights hung across buildings. Music leaked from every direction. People who normally rushed past each other were suddenly stopping, talking, posing for photos as if time had paused just for this one night.

I wasn’t planning to attend.

Karti dragged me.

“Bro, if you sit in the hostel again, I’ll personally cancel your social life certificate.”

“I never applied for one.”

“Exactly. That’s the problem.”

And before I could argue, I was already walking beside him toward the main ground.

—

Festivals do something strange to familiar places.

They soften them.

The same steps, the same trees, the same classrooms — everything looked different under warm lights and evening noise. Even people felt different. Less guarded. Less rushed. Like they had temporarily stepped out of routine.

Karti disappeared within minutes, pulled into a group discussion about food stalls and performances.

I stood near the edge, watching.

Not really searching for anyone.

Just observing.

And then… I saw her.

Aadhya.

For a second, I didn’t recognise her.

Not because she looked different.

But because she looked… complete in a way I hadn’t seen before.

She was walking toward the ground entrance, adjusting the edge of her dupatta as she moved. The lights caught softly on the fabric, the colours shifting each time she stepped forward. Her hair was slightly open, moving with the evening breeze. No hurry. No effort to stand out.

And yet, she did.

Without trying.

It wasn’t about the outfit.

It was the way she carried herself.

Calm. Unaware of attention. Present in her own space.

That’s what held my gaze.

I had seen her every day for months.

In classrooms. In corridors. In messages. In late-night calls.

But this version of her felt… new.

Not unfamiliar.

Just deeper.

Like I was noticing layers that routine had hidden.

She spotted me then.

Paused.

A small smile.

And suddenly, everything else blurred.

She walked over, slower now, as if the crowd had thinned just for that moment.

“You came?” she asked.

“Yeah,” I said. “Karti kidnapped me.”

She laughed softly. “Good. Otherwise you would’ve missed this.”

“Missed what?”

She gestured around. “This version of college.”

I looked around again.

Lights. Music. People. Energy.

Then back at her.

“…yeah,” I said quietly. “Maybe.”

There was a pause.

Not awkward.

Just full.

I realised I was still looking at her.

And she noticed.

“What?” she asked, tilting her head slightly.

“Nothing.”

“Liar.”

I smiled.

“You look different today.”

She raised an eyebrow. “Different good or different strange?”

“…different real.”

That caught her off guard.

For a second, she didn’t reply.

Then she looked away, pretending to watch something happening on stage.

“People usually just say ‘nice’ and move on,” she said.

“I’m not people.”

She glanced back, eyes softer now.

“I know.”

—

We walked through the stalls after that.

No plan. No direction.

Just moving.

Talking about random things. Food. Performances. How half the decorations would disappear by morning. How everyone suddenly acted like photographers during events.

At one point, a group called her for a picture.

She hesitated, then turned to me.

“Don’t run away,” she said. “I’ll be back.”

“I’m not going anywhere.”

She went.

And I stood there, watching her blend into a group I didn’t know.

Laughing.

Talking.

Looking… lighter.

That’s when it hit me.

I was seeing her life beyond me.

Friends I hadn’t met. Conversations I wasn’t part of. Moments where I was just another presence, not the center.

And strangely…

It didn’t hurt.

It grounded me.

Because she wasn’t becoming part of my world alone.

I was slowly understanding hers too.

—

Later, we sat near the quieter side of the ground.

Music still reached us, but softer.

The crowd had thinned a little.

Karti appeared, stole my drink, insulted my fashion sense, then disappeared again without explanation.

She laughed watching him leave.

“He’s chaos,” she said.

“He’s necessary chaos.”

“Hmm.”

She grew quiet after that.

Not distant.

Just thoughtful.

“What?” I asked.

She hesitated. “Nothing.”

“You always say that when it’s something.”

She smiled faintly.

“…this feels different,” she said finally.

“The festival?”

“No.” She shook her head slowly. “Us. Being here. Like this.”

I didn’t reply immediately.

Because I felt it too.

Not louder.

Just clearer.

“I know,” I said.

Silence again.

But this time, it felt heavier.

Not uncomfortable.

Just… aware.

Of time.

Of presence.

Of how moments like this don’t repeat exactly the same way.

She looked ahead, voice softer now.

“I don’t usually come to things like this.”

“Why?”

“Too many people. Too much noise.”

“Then why today?”

She smiled slightly.

“…I wanted to see how it feels.”

“And?”

She looked at me.

“For the first time… it doesn’t feel like noise.”

That stayed.

Long after the music faded.

Long after the lights dimmed.

Long after the crowd dispersed.

Because in that moment, I understood something quietly.

It wasn’t the festival.

It wasn’t the lights.

It wasn’t the crowd.

It was us.

Standing in the middle of everything ordinary…

and still feeling like something meaningful was beginning.
