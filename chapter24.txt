

Some days arrive with noise.

Music echoing through corridors.
Strings of lights across buildings.
Girls in bright colours.
Boys pretending they dressed up for “culture” and not for photos.

College festival day.

The campus didn’t look like the same place.

Even the air felt different.

Lighter. Charged.

Karti had been excited since morning.

“Bro, today is the day,” he declared while adjusting his kurta.

“For what?”

“For nothing specific. Just… vibes.”

“You sound unemployed.”

He ignored me.

—

I wasn’t looking for anything.

I told myself that.

Until I saw her.

She was walking toward the main stage area.

Slow steps.

Not dramatic.

Not trying to be seen.

And yet…

everything around her felt quieter.

She was wearing a deep maroon half-saree. Traditional, but simple. The fabric moved gently with the wind. Her hair was left open, slightly curled at the ends. A small pair of earrings caught the evening light when she turned her head.

No heavy makeup.

No exaggerated effort.

Just… her.

And for a second, I forgot how to breathe normally.

Karti followed my line of sight.

“Oh.”

That was all he said.

Then, very calmly,

“Okay. I understand.”

“Shut up.”

“I didn’t say anything.”

“You didn’t have to.”

He grinned.

—

She noticed us standing near the steps.

Walked over.

Calm as always.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You’re both staring.”

Karti raised his hands immediately.

“Not me.”

She looked at me.

I tried to sound normal.

“You look… different.”

Her eyebrow lifted slightly.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Different good or different concerning?”

“Good,” I said, maybe too quickly.

She smiled.

Not wide.

Just enough.

—

The stage performances started.

Music.

Clapping.

Random cheering.

We stood together near the side.

People kept passing by, greeting her, complimenting her outfit.

I noticed something uncomfortable.

I didn’t like how many people were noticing.

Not because of attention.

But because I suddenly understood how visible she was.

And how easily someone else could see what I had been seeing for months.

—

“Relax,” Karti muttered quietly beside me.

“I am relaxed.”

“You’re clenching your jaw.”

I exhaled.

He smirked.

“Possessive much?”

I shot him a look.

“Careful,” he added softly. “Don’t let it show too obviously.”

That sentence stuck.

Because I hadn’t even realised it was showing.

—

Later, after the performances, we walked toward the quieter side of campus.

Fairy lights lined the path.

Music was distant now.

The sky was dark but clear.

She walked slightly ahead, adjusting her dupatta.

Then slowed so we were beside each other again.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You’ve been quiet.”

“Just watching.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Watching what?”

“Everything.”

She gave me a knowing look.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You don’t like crowded days.”

“I don’t like not knowing where I stand.”

The words slipped out before I could filter them.

She paused mid-step.

Looked at me.

For a second, neither of us moved.

Music echoed faintly behind us.

Lights blurred in the background.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “And where do you think you stand?”

The question wasn’t teasing.

It wasn’t light.

It was steady.

Honest.

And I realised something in that moment.

All these months, I had been afraid of naming it.

But fear hadn’t made it disappear.

It had only made it heavier.

“I don’t know,” I admitted.

“And that scares you?”

“Yes.”

She held my gaze.

No smile.

No nervousness.

Just quiet understanding.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “It doesn’t scare me.”

That answer changed something.

Not loudly.

But deeply.

—

A group of juniors ran past us, laughing loudly, breaking the moment.

She looked away first.

But I could feel the shift between us.

It wasn’t casual anymore.

It wasn’t undefined.

It was standing at the edge of something.

—

Later that night, after everyone left, after Karti disappeared with friends, after lights dimmed…

I stood alone near the stage for a moment.

And replayed her words.

“It doesn’t scare me.”

That meant she wasn’t running.

She wasn’t uncertain.

She wasn’t treating this like something temporary.

And suddenly, the only person still hesitating…

was me.

—

When I reached the hostel, my phone buzzed.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You never finished your sentence.”

I stared at the message.

<strong>Me:</strong> “Which one?”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Where you think you stand.”

I exhaled slowly.

This was the edge.

The point where things either move forward…

or stay suspended.

I typed.

Deleted.

Typed again.

Finally sent:

<strong>Me:</strong> “Closer than I expected.”

Three dots appeared instantly.

Paused.

Came back.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Good.”

Just one word.

But it carried weight.

—

Festival days are loud.

Bright.

Temporary.

But sometimes…

they illuminate things that were already there.

Standing on that campus under fading lights, I understood something clearly.

This wasn’t just comfort anymore.

It wasn’t just routine.

It wasn’t just habit.

It was something building toward a confession neither of us had said yet.

And for the first time…

I wasn’t sure I wanted to stop it from happening.

Because maybe…

the next step wasn’t fear.

Maybe it was courage.

And maybe…

that courage was closer than I thought.
