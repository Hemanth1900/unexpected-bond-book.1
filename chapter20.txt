

Distance doesn’t arrive dramatically.

It comes in smaller forms.

A reply that used to take seconds now takes minutes.
A call that used to last an hour ends in twenty.
A terrace that once felt like habit slowly becomes optional.

Nothing loud.

Just quieter.



It started with messages.

Not cold.

Not rude.

Just… shorter.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Reached?”
<strong>Me:</strong> “Yeah.”
<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Okay.”

Earlier, “okay” would become a joke.
A random complaint.
A pointless debate.

Now it ended there.

I told myself I was imagining it.

But the pattern repeated.



In class, she still sat nearby.

Still asked doubts.

Still smiled.

But something was measured now.

Like she had drawn an invisible line I hadn’t noticed being placed.



One afternoon, I saw her near the canteen.

She was standing with someone I didn’t recognise.

Tall. Casual. Laughing easily.

She was laughing too.

Not politely.

Genuinely.

I slowed down without meaning to.

Karti, walking beside me, noticed immediately.

“Who’s that?”

“No idea,” I said.

“Doesn’t look like ‘no idea’.”

“I said I don’t know.”

He glanced at me sideways.

“Hmm.”

We walked past them.

She noticed me.

Her expression didn’t change.

Just a small wave.

Normal.

I nodded back.

Kept walking.



“Why didn’t you stop?” Karti asked once we were out of earshot.

“She was talking.”

“So?”

“So I didn’t want to interrupt.”

He looked unconvinced.

“Or you didn’t want to see it.”

“See what?”

“Her laughing with someone else.”

I didn’t respond.

Because that sentence had landed too accurately.



That evening, she messaged like usual.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You left quickly.”

<strong>Me:</strong> “Yeah.”

Pause.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You saw Arjun, right?”

Arjun.

So that was his name.

<strong>Me:</strong> “Yeah.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “He joined our elective. We were discussing assignment.”

Simple explanation.

Nothing suspicious.

Nothing wrong.

And yet…

<strong>Me:</strong> “Oh.”

That was all I sent.

Three dots appeared.

Disappeared.

Appeared again.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You sound different.”

I stared at the screen longer than I should have.

<strong>Me:</strong> “No.”

Another pause.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Okay.”

That “okay” again.

It didn’t accuse.

But it didn’t reach either.



The next few days were strange.

Not distant.

But not the same.

She didn’t initiate terrace conversations.

Didn’t call randomly at night.

Didn’t ask, “What are you thinking?” like she used to.

And I didn’t ask either.

Because I didn’t want to sound insecure.

Didn’t want to admit that something as simple as seeing her laugh with someone else had unsettled me.



One evening, she walked beside me after class.

“Are we okay?” she asked quietly.

The question wasn’t dramatic.

It was calm.

And that made it heavier.

“Yeah,” I said.

“Then why does it feel like we’re not?”

I didn’t answer immediately.

Because I knew the real reason.

Awareness.

Fear.

Jealousy I didn’t want to admit.

And the constant thought:

What if she finds someone simpler?

Someone less complicated.

Someone who doesn’t overthink everything.

“I’ve just been busy,” I said again.

She looked at me for a long second.

“You always say that when you don’t want to talk.”

There was no anger in her voice.

Just disappointment.

And that hurt more.



“I’m not trying to avoid you,” I said.

“Then don’t.”

The simplicity of that response hit harder than any argument.

She wasn’t asking for explanations.

Just presence.



That night, I replayed everything.

Her laugh at the canteen.

Her shorter messages.

Her question: “Are we okay?”

Maybe she wasn’t pulling away.

Maybe she was adjusting.

To the version of me that had started stepping back first.



The next day, I saw her again with Arjun.

This time closer.

Discussing something on her phone.

He leaned slightly toward her.

Not too close.

Just enough to make my chest tighten unexpectedly.

She looked up and saw me.

For a second, something flickered across her face.

Uncertainty.

Maybe she noticed my expression.

Maybe she didn’t.

But I didn’t stop.

I kept walking.



Karti caught up later.

“You’re doing this wrong,” he said.

“Doing what?”

“Acting like you don’t care.”

“I don’t.”

He laughed softly.

“That’s the problem. You care too much.”

I stayed silent.

Because for the first time…

that felt true.



Distance doesn’t break things immediately.

It stretches them.

Gently.

Slowly.

Until one day you realise something that once felt effortless now requires effort.

And effort means something changed.

Standing on the hostel terrace that night alone, I realised something I hadn’t wanted to admit.

It wasn’t Arjun.

It wasn’t the comment in class.

It wasn’t even her shorter replies.

It was me.

I had stepped back first.

Out of fear.

Out of caution.

Out of not wanting to complicate something that felt too good.

And now…

I was watching the space grow.

And I didn’t know how to close it without exposing everything I had tried so hard to hide.

Because the truth was simple.

Seeing her laugh with someone else didn’t hurt because of him.

It hurt because I wasn’t sure where I stood anymore.

And that uncertainty…

felt louder than any argument could have.
