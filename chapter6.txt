

New semesters always come with a strange kind of optimism.
Not the loud kind people post about, but the quiet promise everyone makes to themselves — this time I’ll do things better.

I had made that promise too.

Study more.
Sleep on time.
Stay less distracted.
Stay less… involved.

By the end of the first night, I already knew I wouldn’t keep any of it.

The hostel was louder than usual. Bags dragged across floors, doors slammed open, people shouting names from one corridor to another as if no time had passed between the last semester and this one.

Karti was lying on his bed, one leg hanging off the side, scrolling through his phone like he had returned to exactly the same moment he had paused months ago.

“Machaa,” he said without looking up, “new semester… new failures incoming.”

I dropped my bag near the table. “You failed last sem?”

He turned, offended. “I didn’t fail. I strategically postponed success.”

I laughed. It felt normal. Comfortingly normal.

Outside our room, someone was arguing about lost notes. Down the hall, a group was already planning a late-night tea run. The same smells, the same noise, the same routine wrapping itself back around all of us like nothing had changed.

But something had.

At least for me.

Karti finally sat up and looked at me properly. “You’re quiet.”

“I just came da.”

“No… not that. Since last month. You’ve been… somewhere else.”

I shrugged it off, opening my bag. “Overthinking.”

He didn’t respond immediately. Just watched for a second, then smirked.

“Phone la yaaru?”

I threw a pillow at him. “Shut up.”

He laughed like he had expected that answer.

The conversation moved on. Professors. Attendance. Timetable rumors. People we hoped wouldn’t sit near us again. The usual nonsense that fills hostel nights before a new term begins.

But somewhere between those words, my mind kept drifting.

Tomorrow.

Same class. Same benches. Same people.

Her.

I didn’t say it out loud. Didn’t even let the thought fully form. But it was there, sitting quietly behind everything else.

And for the first time, I realised something strange.

I wasn’t curious about the semester.

I was curious about seeing her again.



Morning came with the sound of alarms that nobody turned off.

Half the hostel woke up late. The other half pretended to be awake while still lying in bed. Bathrooms flooded. Someone yelled about missing socks. Someone else was already wearing an ID card like a badge of responsibility that would fade in three days.

Karti dragged me to class before I could delay.

“First day da. Sit in front. Fake sincerity.”

We didn’t.

We took our usual place — middle rows, near the window. Close enough to pay attention, far enough to escape.

Students filled the room in clusters. Familiar faces returning like pieces of a paused story resuming from where it left off.

And then she walked in.

No announcement. No dramatic entry.

Just… there.

Aadhya.

Her hair slightly shorter than before. Same calm expression. Same way of scanning the room before choosing a seat.

She sat two benches ahead. Slightly to the right.

Not far. Not near.

Exactly where she had always been.

I didn’t realise I had been watching until Karti nudged me.

“Attendance miss aagidum da.”

I looked away immediately, pretending to open my notebook.

But my focus wasn’t on the page.

It was on the familiarity of her presence.

Nothing had changed.

And yet, everything felt different.

The professor walked in, already speaking before the class settled.

New syllabus. Internal marks. Deadlines. Warnings we would ignore in a week.

People whispered. Chairs scraped. Pens clicked. The noise of a new beginning pretending to be serious.

And through all of it, I was aware of one thing.

Her.

The way she leaned forward when listening.
The way she wrote in short pauses instead of continuously.
The way she turned slightly when someone behind her spoke.

These were details I had no reason to notice.

And yet I remembered them.

Karti leaned closer and whispered, “Bro… new semester motivation or old semester continuation?”

I smirked. “Shut up and write.”

But he noticed.

Of course he did.

Best friends always do.



Break time.

The classroom emptied into the corridor like a released breath. People laughing louder than necessary, reintroducing themselves to routines they had missed without admitting it.

We stepped out too.

Karti immediately got pulled into a group discussing last semester’s disasters. Backlogs. Viva failures. One guy dramatically explaining how he studied “whole night” for an exam he still barely passed.

I stood there, half listening.

And then I felt it.

That strange awareness again.

Not someone calling me. Not someone touching me.

Just… being noticed.

I turned slightly.

Aadhya was standing a few steps away, talking to two classmates. Mid-sentence, she glanced in our direction.

Our eyes met for a second.

No reaction. No surprise.

Just recognition.

The kind you give someone who has been part of your everyday space for long enough.

Then she looked away, continuing her conversation.

That should have been nothing.

But it wasn’t.

Because in that second, I realised something simple and terrifying at the same time.

She knew I existed.

Not as a classmate.

Not as a name on attendance.

But as someone she had already noticed before.

And that changed the air around everything.

Karti snapped his fingers in front of my face. “Earth to Charan.”

“Huh?”

“Bro, you’ve been staring into space for five minutes. New semester resolution — stop daydreaming.”

I shook my head. “Nothing.”

He narrowed his eyes like he didn’t believe me, then smiled.

“Something is there.”

I didn’t argue.

Because for the first time… there actually was.



Classes continued. Subjects piled up. Timetables settled into place.

But beneath all that structure, something had quietly restarted.

Not a relationship.
Not a confession.
Not even a conversation.

Just a presence.

Back in the same room.
Back in the same routine.
Back in the same silent distance.

And this time, I wasn’t just noticing it.

I was waiting for it.

Waiting for the moment that would turn this from familiarity… into something real.
