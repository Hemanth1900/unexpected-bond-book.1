



Some connections don’t begin with a moment. They begin with repetition.

The same name appearing every day.
The same voice settling into your routine.
The same person slowly becoming part of the structure of your thoughts.

That’s how it happened with her.

Not through a dramatic conversation. Not through a confession. Just through consistency. Through the quiet comfort of knowing she would be there — somewhere between the start and end of my day.

And before I realised it, I had started expecting her.

Not in a way that demanded attention, but in the way your mind prepares itself for something familiar. Like a favorite song playing softly in the background — not loud enough to interrupt, but impossible to ignore once it stops.

I began to notice the absence more than the presence.

Days when we spoke less felt strangely unfinished. Conversations that ended too soon stayed with me longer than they should have. I would replay them, not because they were extraordinary, but because they felt incomplete.

And somewhere inside, I had started waiting.

Waiting without admitting it.
Waiting without needing to be told.

That was new for me.

I had always been someone who stayed distant, careful not to let anyone become a necessity. People came and went. Conversations faded. Attachments loosened over time. That had always been the rhythm of my life.

But with her, the rhythm changed.

She didn’t push her way into my world. She didn’t try to be important. She didn’t even notice the shift happening inside me.

And maybe that’s why it felt real.

Because nothing was forced.

It was just two people speaking, sharing, existing… and somewhere between those ordinary exchanges, something began to take shape.

Something I couldn’t name yet.

There were moments I caught myself wanting to tell her things I usually kept to myself. Thoughts that felt too personal. Feelings I had never put into words before.

Not because I trusted easily.

But because with her, silence didn’t feel safer than honesty.

And that confused me.

Because when you start opening up without fear, it means someone has already crossed a distance you didn’t realise existed.

I started noticing how much of my day I carried back to her. Small things. Unimportant details. Fragments of conversations. Passing thoughts. Things I wouldn’t normally share with anyone.

But with her, I wanted to.

Not for validation. Not for sympathy.

Just to be heard.

And she listened.

Not with exaggerated reactions or forced interest — just quietly, attentively, as if everything I said mattered simply because I said it.

That kind of attention is rare.

And once you experience it, you begin to depend on it without meaning to.

That was the moment I realised I was no longer just used to her presence.

I was becoming attached to it.

Not loudly. Not dramatically.

But in the way your mind starts reserving space for someone without asking permission.

She had become part of my normal.

And normal things feel safe… until the day they’re not there anymore.

That thought arrived suddenly, without warning.

What if this changed?

What if she stopped being part of my routine?

What if one day, the conversations ended… and nothing replaced them?

The idea felt distant, almost unrealistic.

But it stayed.

And for the first time, I understood something quietly unsettling.

The more natural a connection feels, the more fragile it becomes.

Because you stop preparing for its absence.

And maybe… I had already reached that point.

Maybe I had already let her become something I didn’t know how to lose.

Because she wasn’t just another person anymore.

She had become a feeling I carried.

A presence I noticed even when she wasn’t there.
A voice my mind searched for in silence.
A habit I didn’t remember forming — but couldn’t imagine breaking.

And that’s the dangerous part about people who arrive gently.

You don’t see them changing your life.

You only realise it when the thought of them leaving feels heavier than it should.

I never called it love.

Not then.

It felt too early. Too undefined. Too quiet to be given a word that big.

But she had already crossed into a place no one else had reached in a long time.

Not my past.
Not just my present.

But somewhere in between — where memories start forming before moments even end.

And I think that’s when I understood something I had always avoided admitting.

Some people don’t become important through promises.

They become important through presence.

Through showing up.
Through staying.
Through becoming part of the everyday without asking to be noticed.

She didn’t try to be unforgettable.

And that’s exactly why she became impossible to ignore.

Even now, when I look back, I can’t point to the exact day things changed.

There was no turning point. No confession. No moment that announced itself.

Just a slow shift.

From talking… to waiting.
From noticing… to caring.
From comfort… to fear of losing it.

And somewhere in that quiet transition, she became more than someone I spoke to.

She became someone I carried.

Not loudly. Not obviously.

But in the quiet corners of my thoughts — where only the people who truly matter end up staying.

And I think… that was the real beginning.

Not of love.

But of the possibility of it.

And sometimes, that’s even more terrifying.
