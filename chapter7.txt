

The first few days of the semester passed exactly how they always do — noisy, disorganised, full of promises no one really planned to keep.

Assignments were mentioned but not started. Attendance mattered but not enough. Everyone spoke about studying seriously this time, and everyone laughed at it at the same time.

And somewhere in between all of it, routines quietly formed again.

Benches became permanent. Friend groups settled back into shape. Timings adjusted. Shortcuts across campus were remembered without thinking.

So did people.

Aadhya was no longer just someone in the room.

She was part of the pattern.

Not always talking. Not always near. But present in a way that didn’t feel new anymore.

And that presence had started to feel… normal.



It happened on a day that didn’t feel important.

No rain. No special class. No reason for anything memorable.

Just an afternoon lecture that got cancelled.

Half the class left immediately. The other half stayed back, pretending to be productive while actually scrolling through their phones or complaining about the timetable.

Karti got dragged away by someone from another section, leaving me alone in the classroom for once.

I stayed back without thinking.

Notebook open. Pen in hand. No intention of actually studying.

Just… sitting.

Aadhya was a few benches ahead, flipping through her notes. No one else near her. The classroom quieter than usual.

For a moment, I considered leaving.

Then she turned.

“Charan?”

It took me a second to realise she was talking to me.

“Hm?”

“You have last semester’s micro notes… right?”

I blinked. “Yeah… I think so.”

“Can I see? Mine are incomplete.”

Simple. Direct. Normal.

And yet, something about hearing my name from her felt unfamiliar in a way I couldn’t explain.

I walked forward, handing over the notebook.

She didn’t rush. Sat properly. Started reading. Occasionally turning pages slower when something caught her attention.

“Your handwriting is very… disciplined,” she said.

I laughed softly. “That’s a new way of calling it boring.”

She smiled, not looking up. “No. It’s neat. Easy to follow.”

Silence settled again.

Not awkward. Just quiet.

The kind that doesn’t demand words.

She closed the notebook after a minute and handed it back.

“You actually study ah?”

“Rarely.”

She laughed — properly this time.

Not loudly. Just enough for the sound to stay in the air.

“I thought so.”

“Why?”

“You listen in class,” she said. “But you don’t look like someone who panics about marks.”

I leaned against the bench. “Observation skills.”

She shrugged. “I notice things.”

I believed her.

Because I had noticed the same about her.

“You stay in hostel?” she asked.

“Yeah. Block C.”

“Hmm.”

“You?”

“Day scholar.”

Of course.

That explained the punctuality. The organized notes. The calm.

Conversations continued slowly. No rush. No effort to impress.

Just small exchanges that somehow kept going.

Timetable.
Professors.
How chaotic the first week always feels.
How everyone promises to change and never does.

At some point, we forgot why the conversation started.

And just kept talking.



“What were you like in school?” she asked suddenly.

I paused. “Quiet.”

She looked up this time. “Now also quiet.”

“Comfortably quiet.”

She nodded like she understood the difference.

“I was the opposite,” she said. “Always talking. Always getting into trouble.”

“I can’t imagine that.”

“People change.”

“Or they just become careful.”

She didn’t respond immediately.

Just smiled slightly.

That pause felt longer than it actually was.

And in that moment, I realised something strange.

Talking to her didn’t feel like effort.

No overthinking. No planning words.

Just… conversation.

Like it had always existed and we were simply continuing it.



“Machaaa!”

Karti’s voice broke the moment like a stone dropped into still water.

He walked in, stopped mid-step when he saw us, and his expression changed instantly.

“Ohhh,” he dragged the sound unnecessarily. “Serious academic discussion ah?”

I rolled my eyes. “Notes.”

Aadhya smiled politely, already closing the notebook.

Karti leaned against the desk dramatically. “Charan sharing notes means something is definitely wrong.”

“Get lost.”

He laughed, turning to her. “I’m Karthik. Everyone calls me Karti. Best friend. Unfortunately.”

“Aadhya,” she replied.

“I know,” he said immediately, then pretended to correct himself. “I mean… attendance list la pathiruken.”

I wanted to kick him.

She just nodded, amused.

The moment had shifted. Not ruined. Just changed shape.

She stood up, adjusting her bag. “Thanks for the notes.”

“Anytime,” I said before thinking.

She gave a small nod and walked out.

No dramatic pause. No looking back.

Just left.



Karti waited two full seconds before turning to me with the widest grin possible.

“Dei.”

I ignored him.

“Dei.”

“Shut up.”

“Conversation ah?”

“Notes.”

“Five minutes notes… fifteen minutes smiling.”

I grabbed my bag. “You’re jobless.”

He followed anyway. “Machaa… something is happening.”

“Nothing is happening.”

He didn’t argue.

Just walked beside me, hands behind his head, like he was enjoying watching me deny something I hadn’t even fully admitted yet.



The rest of the day passed normally.

Classes. Corridor noise. Hostel chatter.

But that conversation stayed.

Not because it was deep.

Not because it was emotional.

But because it felt easy.

Effortless.

Like talking to her didn’t require me to be someone else.

And that’s when it hit me.

The dangerous part wasn’t liking her.

The dangerous part was getting used to how natural it felt when she was around.

Because once something feels natural…

You start missing it the moment it isn’t there.

And I had a feeling this was only the beginning.
