



I didn’t notice when her presence stopped feeling new and started feeling necessary.

It wasn’t sudden. Nothing about her ever was. She entered spaces quietly, like a familiar scent you couldn’t trace back to its source. At first, she was just another person I spoke to, another voice among many. But slowly, without effort, she began to stay longer in my thoughts than anyone else did.

There were days when nothing important happened between us. No long conversations. No emotional confessions. Just small exchanges, scattered through ordinary hours. And yet, those were the days I felt her absence the most.

I started noticing patterns.

The way my mood shifted when I saw her name appear. The way silence felt lighter when she was around. The way time moved differently — slower, softer — whenever we spoke.

It wasn’t attachment. Not yet. But it was no longer nothing.

Some people don’t enter your life loudly. They don’t ask for attention or announce their importance. They simply exist beside you, and over time, their presence becomes something your mind starts looking for without permission.

She was becoming that.

I didn’t wait for her messages. I wasn’t that obvious with myself. But I checked my phone more often. I reread conversations. I caught myself smiling at things that weren’t meant to be special.

And then pretending it meant nothing.

Because acknowledging it would make it real.

And real things have a way of leaving.

There was comfort in how effortless everything felt. No pressure to impress. No fear of being misunderstood. With her, words didn’t feel like something I had to arrange carefully. They just came, and she understood.

That understanding scared me more than distance ever could.

Because when someone understands you without asking, they start seeing parts of you that even you avoid.

There were moments I caught myself watching her — not intentionally, not in a way I could explain. Just noticing. The way she listened. The way she paused before responding. The way her silence sometimes said more than her words.

It felt familiar.

Dangerously familiar.

Like remembering a place you’ve never been before.

And slowly, she began occupying the quiet spaces in my day. The pauses between tasks. The moments before sleep. The seconds when nothing demanded my attention.

She was there.

Not physically. Not always speaking. Just… present.

That’s when I realised something had shifted.

Her presence was no longer an addition to my day.

It had become part of its structure.

And the strange thing was — she never asked for it. Never tried to stay. Never demanded importance.

She simply existed.

And somehow, that was enough for me to start making space.

Space in my routine.
Space in my thoughts.
Space in the parts of my life I had always kept guarded.

It wasn’t love.

Not yet.

But it was the first time someone’s presence felt like something I would notice if it disappeared.

And that was the beginning of something I didn’t yet understand — but was already afraid of losing.
