

Some days don’t feel important while they’re happening.

They don’t arrive with heavy conversations or moments that demand to be remembered. They just pass — quietly, gently — leaving behind a feeling you only notice later.

That day felt like that.

Ordinary.

Familiar.

Safe.

And maybe that’s why I remember it so clearly.



It was late afternoon when classes ended. The sky had that dull, tired shade of blue that comes after a long day. People were already planning dinner, some rushing to tuition, others dragging themselves back to the hostel like survivors of a battlefield.

Karti and I walked out together, arguing about something pointless — probably assignments, probably life, probably nothing.

“Bro, I’m telling you,” he said dramatically, “this semester is personal. They’re targeting me.”

“You say that every semester.”

“Because every semester they prove me right.”

I shook my head, smiling despite myself.

He stopped near the steps, looking at his phone. “I’m going to the canteen. Coming?”

“You go. I’ll come later.”

He raised an eyebrow. “Later… or after talking to someone?”

“Shut up.”

He laughed and walked away.

I stood there for a moment, not moving.

Because he wasn’t wrong.



Aadhya had become… routine.

Not in a boring way.

In a comforting way.

Like something your day naturally builds around without effort.

We didn’t plan conversations anymore. They just happened.

A random message.

A passing call.

A shared complaint.

A silence that didn’t need filling.

And slowly, without either of us noticing, we had stopped trying.

Trying to impress.
Trying to explain.
Trying to be careful.

We just… existed.



That evening, I was in my room, lying on the bed, scrolling aimlessly.

My phone buzzed.

Aadhya: “Did you eat?”

I smiled before replying.

Me: “No. Thinking.”

Aadhya: “Dangerous activity.”

Me: “Very.”

There was a pause.

Then—

Aadhya: “You sound quieter these days.”

I stared at that message longer than I should have.

Me: “Do I?”

Aadhya: “Hmm.”

Another pause.

Aadhya: “Not distant… just quieter.”

I didn’t know what to say.

Because she was right.

I had been thinking more.

Feeling more.

Not just about her… but about everything.



Later that night, I went up to the terrace.

The air was cool. Someone nearby was talking loudly on the phone. A dog barked somewhere in the distance. The city lights blinked lazily like they were too tired to stay bright.

I leaned against the railing.

And for the first time in days… I wasn’t thinking.

Just breathing.

Just existing.

My phone rang.

Her.

I picked up.

“Hello?”

“Hey,” she said softly.

“You okay?”

“Yeah… just wanted to talk.”

I didn’t respond immediately.

Not because I didn’t know what to say.

But because I didn’t feel the need to rush.

We stayed silent for a few seconds.

And somehow… it wasn’t awkward.

It felt normal.

Comfortable.

Like both of us knew the other was there.

“That lab today was exhausting,” she finally said.

“Yeah. Karti almost fought with the lab assistant.”

She laughed quietly.

“I can imagine.”

“And you?” I asked. “Finished your readings?”

“Half,” she said. “Then I gave up.”

“Why?”

“I don’t know… mind wasn’t there.”

“Where was it?”

She didn’t reply immediately.

Then—

“Here, I guess.”

Something in my chest shifted.

Not loudly.

Just… enough.



We spoke for a long time after that.

About nothing.

About everything.

Assignments.
Classes.
People.
Random thoughts that didn’t need meaning.

Her voice stayed calm. Soft. Present.

And slowly, the noise around me faded.

The terrace.

The sounds.

The world.

Everything blurred into the background.

It was just her.

And me.

And the space between words.



At some point, she said quietly,

“Do you ever feel like… some people just become part of your day without asking?”

I didn’t answer immediately.

Because I knew what she meant.

I felt it too.

“Yeah,” I said finally.

A pause.

Then she added, almost like a confession she didn’t mean to make,

“And if they disappear… the day feels incomplete.”

My grip tightened slightly on the phone.

I looked up at the sky.

It was darker now.

Calmer.

Like it was listening.

“I get that,” I said softly.

We didn’t say anything after that.

We didn’t need to.

Because some things… once spoken… don’t require explanation.



When the call ended, I stayed there for a while.

Not moving.

Not thinking.

Just letting the silence settle.

And that’s when I realised something.

Nothing dramatic had happened.

No confession.
No promise.
No moment that would look important from the outside.

And yet…

Everything felt different.

Not heavier.

Not confusing.

Just… deeper.

Like the connection had crossed a line neither of us had drawn.

Not into love.

Not yet.

But into something close enough to feel real.

Something that didn’t need words to exist.

Something that didn’t ask for attention.

Something that simply stayed.

And for the first time…

I didn’t wonder where this was going.

I just let it be.

Because sometimes, comfort itself becomes the story.

And sometimes…

that’s where change begins.
