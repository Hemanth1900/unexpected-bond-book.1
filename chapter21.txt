

Some conversations are postponed.

Not avoided — just delayed.
You tell yourself you’ll talk when things feel clearer.
When the timing is right.
When emotions settle.

But sometimes… they arrive before you’re ready.

—

It happened after class.

The corridor was half empty. Most people had already left. Karti had disappeared somewhere, and I was packing my bag slowly, pretending to look for something.

She walked in.

Not rushing.

Not hesitant either.

Just… certain.

“Charan.”

I looked up.

“Hmm?”

“We need to talk.”

Simple.

No tension in her voice.

No accusation.

And yet my chest tightened instantly.

“Okay,” I said.

We stepped outside. The late afternoon sun was softer now. Students moved past us, laughing, arguing, existing like nothing in the world had shifted.

She leaned against the railing.

I stood beside her.

Silence stretched for a few seconds.

Then—

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You’re not here like before.”

I frowned. “What?”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You’re present… but not really. You listen, but you don’t stay. You reply, but you don’t continue.”

I didn’t respond.

Because I knew she was right.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Did something change?”

I hesitated.

“Nothing changed.”

She let out a small breath — not frustration, not relief.

Just… acknowledgement.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Then why does it feel like I’m talking to a version of you that’s trying to stay careful all the time?”

That word.

Careful.

It described everything.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “We used to just talk. Randomly. Stupidly. Honestly.”

I looked away.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Now you think before every sentence. Like you’re filtering yourself.”

She wasn’t wrong.

And hearing it from her made it impossible to deny.

“I just didn’t want to complicate things,” I said quietly.

She turned fully toward me.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Who said it was complicated?”

I met her eyes for the first time since the conversation began.

Because I didn’t have an answer.

Because I didn’t even know when I had started believing that.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Charan… I never asked for anything to change.”

Her voice was softer now.

Not defensive.

Not demanding.

Just honest.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “I just wanted things to stay how they were.”

That hit harder than anything else.

Because I had been the one changing.

—

“I thought…” I started, then stopped.

She waited.

I exhaled slowly.

“I thought if people started noticing… it would become something else.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Something else… or something real?”

The question caught me off guard.

I didn’t respond.

Because I didn’t know.

Or maybe I did, and I wasn’t ready to say it.

—

She looked away for a second, watching students walk past.

Then said quietly,

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You know what scared me?”

I turned toward her.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Not that you were quiet.”

A pause.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “That you were leaving slowly… without telling me.”

That sentence settled heavily between us.

Because that’s exactly what I had been doing.

Not intentionally.

But still.

—

“I wasn’t leaving,” I said.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Then stay.”

The simplicity of that response broke something inside me.

No expectations.

No demands.

Just… stay.

—

For a moment, neither of us spoke.

The world continued around us.

But that space — that small, quiet space between us — felt separate.

Important.

—

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “We don’t have to define anything,” she said after a while.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “We don’t have to explain it to anyone.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “But don’t start treating it like something fragile that might break if you touch it.”

I let out a small breath.

Because that’s exactly what I had been doing.

Handling everything carefully.

Thinking too much.

Trying not to ruin something that had been natural.

And in that process… making it unnatural.

—

“I didn’t realise I was doing that,” I admitted.

She smiled faintly.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “I know.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “That’s why I came to talk.”

Not to argue.

Not to accuse.

Just to pull me back before the distance became permanent.

—

We stood there longer than planned.

Talking.

Not about feelings.

Not about definitions.

Just… normal things.

Assignments. Classes. Karti’s nonsense. Hostel food.

And slowly, conversation found its rhythm again.

Not exactly like before.

But closer.

Warmer.

Less guarded.

—

As we walked back toward the main gate, she slowed down slightly.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “We’re okay, right?”

This time the question felt different.

Not uncertain.

Just confirming.

“Yeah,” I said.

And this time… I meant it.

—

That night, I realised something quietly.

Distance doesn’t always grow because of misunderstandings.

Sometimes it grows because of fear.

Fear of naming things.

Fear of losing them.

Fear of wanting them too much.

And maybe…

the only way to stop it…

is to stop running from what it might become.

I didn’t know where this was going.

Didn’t know what it meant yet.

But for the first time in days…

I wasn’t stepping back.

I was staying.

Exactly where she had asked me to.

And maybe that…

was the beginning of something stronger than anything we had before.
