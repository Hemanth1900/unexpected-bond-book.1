

The library always smelled the same.

Old paper. Dust. Air conditioning that worked just enough to keep you awake.

It was quieter than usual that afternoon. Most students were preparing for internals, pretending focus was a personality trait.

I was seated near the back row, a stack of books open in front of me, notes half-written, concentration halfway there.

I didn’t hear her sit down.

But I felt it.

That slight shift in air.

“You ignored me yesterday.”

No greeting.

No smile.

Just that.

I looked up.

Aadhya.

Notebook in hand. Expression calm — but not casual.

“I didn’t ignore you.”

“You did.”

Her tone wasn’t angry.

Just certain.

I leaned back slightly. “When?”

“After class.”

I tried to remember.

I had left quickly. Karti had dragged me somewhere. Or maybe I just… avoided something.

“I was busy.”

“You didn’t even reply to my message.”

I opened my phone instinctively.

There it was.

One unread text.

Aadhya: “You left suddenly?”

I hadn’t responded.

Not intentionally.

But I hadn’t.

“I forgot,” I said quietly.

She held my gaze.

“You don’t usually forget.”

That wasn’t accusation.

That was observation.

And that made it heavier.


---

“I had things on my mind,” I said finally.

“Like?”

“Nothing important.”

Her expression changed slightly.

“You say that a lot.”

I didn’t respond.

Because she was right.


---

Silence filled the space between us.

The library remained calm. Pages turning somewhere nearby. A chair scraping softly across the floor.

But the air between us felt different.

Tighter.


---

“You’ve been pulling back,” she said.

The words were steady.

No drama.

Just truth.

I looked at her.

“What?”

“You talk less. You leave faster. You think more before answering.”

“That’s not pulling back.”

“It feels like it.”

Her voice didn’t rise.

But something inside it shifted.


---

“I’m not,” I said quietly.

“Then what is it?”

I didn’t have an answer.

Because the truth sounded stupid even in my own head.

Fear.

Overthinking.

The awareness that she was becoming important.

And important things can disappear.


---

“I just don’t want to complicate things,” I said carefully.

“Complicate what?”

“This.”

Her eyes searched my face.

“And what is this?”

The question stayed suspended between shelves and silence.

I didn’t say it.

Didn’t define it.

Because once defined, it becomes real.

And real things demand responsibility.


---

“We were just talking,” I said finally.

She leaned back slightly.

“Just talking?”

There was something in her tone now.

Not anger.

But something closer to disappointment.


---

“I didn’t think it needed… weight,” I added.

She looked down at her notebook.

Then back at me.

“Not everything needs weight,” she said softly. “But not everything is nothing either.”

That hit.

Because that’s exactly what I had been doing.

Reducing everything to nothing.

So it wouldn’t matter.


---

“You think too much,” she said quietly.

“You don’t?”

“I do. But I don’t run from it.”

The sentence stayed.


---

I looked away.

To the books.

To the floor.

Anywhere but her eyes.

Because she was seeing too clearly.


---

“I’m not running,” I said.

“Then stop acting like you are.”

Silence again.

Thicker this time.


---

“I don’t want to lose something before it even starts,” I admitted finally.

The words slipped out before I could stop them.

She didn’t react immediately.

Just watched.

“And you think pulling away protects it?”

“I don’t know.”

“That’s honest at least.”


---

A pause.

Then she softened slightly.

“I’m not asking for definitions,” she said. “Or labels.”

“Then what?”

“Consistency.”

The word landed heavier than any confession.


---

“I thought we had that,” I said.

“We did,” she replied.

That past tense.

Small.

But sharp.


---

The library felt colder suddenly.

Or maybe it was just me.


---

“I’m still here,” I said quietly.

“I know,” she replied. “But sometimes you feel distant even when you’re sitting right in front of me.”

That hurt more than I expected.

Because I knew it was true.


---

Another chair scraped somewhere far.

Someone whispered for silence.

The world continued.

But ours had shifted slightly.


---

She closed her notebook slowly.

“I don’t want to guess where I stand,” she said calmly. “That’s exhausting.”

“You don’t have to.”

“Then don’t make me.”

That wasn’t a demand.

It was a boundary.


---

She stood up.

For a second, I thought she’d leave.

But she didn’t.

She just adjusted her bag and looked at me once more.

“I don’t need everything to be certain,” she said. “But I need it to be real.”

Then she walked toward another table.

Not far.

But not beside me either.


---

I stayed there.

Staring at the same page.

Not reading.

Just thinking.

She hadn’t shouted.

Hadn’t accused.

Hadn’t dramatized anything.

She had simply said what she felt.

And I had realised something uncomfortable.

I wasn’t scared of losing her.

I was scared of how much I already didn’t want to.

And fear has a way of making you step back from the very thing you care about.

The problem is—

sometimes stepping back is what actually pushes it away.
