

The rain started without warning.

One moment the sky was just grey. The next, water was falling hard enough to blur the buildings across the campus.

Classes had just ended. Students rushed toward corridors, some laughing, some cursing, some trying to save notebooks from getting soaked.

I stepped out anyway.

Bad decision.

Within seconds, the ground turned slick, the air colder, and visibility dropped.

The bus stop near the gate was already crowded. People pressed under the narrow shade, trying to avoid the rain that kept slanting sideways with the wind.

I moved toward the edge, shaking water off my sleeves.

And that’s when I saw her.

Standing near the pillar, holding her bag close, hair slightly damp at the ends.

Aadhya.

She noticed me almost immediately.

“You’re drenched,” she said, eyebrows lifting.

“Occupational hazard.”

“Of walking in rain?”

“Of not thinking ahead.”

She smiled, shifting slightly to make space.

I stood beside her, close enough to feel the warmth of another presence in the cold air.

The rain only got heavier.

Water hit the metal roof loudly, creating a constant drumming sound that made conversations softer, more private.




“You heading back to hostel?” I asked.

“Yeah. Bus isn’t coming anytime soon though.”

I glanced at the road. Empty.

“Figures.”

For a minute, we just watched the rain.

Students ran across puddles. Someone slipped and laughed. A group under the opposite shed started singing loudly for no reason.

Normal chaos.

But inside the small shade of that bus stop, everything felt… slower.




“You like rain?” she asked suddenly.

“Depends.”

“On?”

“Who I’m stuck with.”

She gave me a look.

“That was a risky answer.”

“Honest one.”

“And?”

“And today it’s tolerable.”

She shook her head, smiling faintly.

“High praise.”




The wind pushed rain inside the shelter.

She stepped back instinctively, closer to me.

Our shoulders brushed for a second.

Neither of us moved immediately.

Then she adjusted her position slightly, pretending to check her phone.

I looked away, pretending to notice the road.

But awareness lingered.




“You always do that,” she said.

“Do what?”

“Pretend nothing happened.”

I blinked. “Nothing did happen.”

She tilted her head slightly. “Exactly.”

I didn’t argue.

Because she wasn’t entirely wrong.




A loud thunder cracked somewhere far.

She flinched slightly, then laughed at herself.

“I hate sudden thunder.”

“You don’t look like someone who scares easily.”

“I don’t. Just… unexpected things.”

“Fair.”

She watched the rain again, expression softer now.

“Rain makes everything feel quieter,” she said. “Even when it’s loud.”

“That makes no sense.”

“It does,” she insisted. “People slow down. Conversations change. Everything feels more… real.”

I looked at her.

“You think too much.”

“You don’t think enough.”

“Balance.”

“Excuse.”

That made me laugh.




A bus passed without stopping, splashing water across the road.

Some people groaned.

She sighed, adjusting her bag strap.

“We might be stuck here a while.”

“Not the worst place.”

She raised an eyebrow. “Why?”

I shrugged. “No assignments. No professors. No Karti.”

“True. That last one is a blessing.”

“He’d be narrating life philosophy by now.”

“Or complaining about rain personally targeting him.”

We both laughed.

The ease returned.

That same familiar comfort.




After a moment, she asked quietly,

“Do you always stay this calm?”

“In chaos?”

“Yeah.”

“Not always.”

“When do you panic?”

I thought about it.

“Mostly when I don’t understand what I’m feeling.”

She looked at me.

Not teasing. Not curious.

Just attentive.

“That’s honest,” she said softly.




Rain softened slightly.

Not stopping, but slowing.

Enough for people to start moving again.

She stepped forward, looking at the road.

“I think I’ll walk,” she said.

“In this?”

“It’s not that bad now.”

“It’ll start again.”

She hesitated.

Then looked at me.

“You coming?”

I nodded.

We stepped out together.

The rain soaked us within seconds, but neither of us ran.

We walked slowly, careful over puddles, sharing the narrow dry path near the edge of the road.

At one point, she slipped slightly.

I caught her wrist instinctively.

She steadied herself quickly.

“Thanks.”

I let go immediately.

But the contact lingered.




We didn’t speak for a while after that.

Just walked.

Side by side.

The kind of silence that didn’t demand filling.

Near the hostel gate, she slowed down.

“I’ll go this way,” she said, pointing toward her block.

“Yeah.”

She hesitated, like she wanted to say something else.

Then simply smiled.

“Today wasn’t bad.”

“Because of rain?”

“Because of company.”

She turned before I could respond.

Walking away, not looking back.




I stood there for a second longer, watching rain ripple across the road.

The air smelled different.

Fresh.

Heavy.

Alive.

That moment hadn’t been dramatic.

No confessions. No big emotions.

Just two people stuck under rain, sharing space, sharing silence, sharing something neither of them named.

But it stayed.

And sometimes…

it’s the ordinary moments that quietly decide everything.
