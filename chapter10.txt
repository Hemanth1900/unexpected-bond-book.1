
Some distances don’t begin with arguments. They begin with hesitation.

That week, nothing visibly changed. Classes went on. Assignments continued. The hostel corridor still echoed at night. Karti still complained about life like it personally offended him. But something inside me had started slowing down. Not because of her — because of myself.

It was a Wednesday evening when I first felt it clearly. We were sitting in the college canteen — me, Karti, and two others from class. Plastic chairs scraping, steel tumblers clinking, someone laughing too loudly from the next table.

I was half-listening to a story Karti was narrating with unnecessary drama when my phone buzzed.

Aadhya.

I didn’t open it immediately.

Karti noticed. “You’re not checking?”

“I will.”

“You usually check before the phone even vibrates.”

I ignored him and unlocked it casually.

Aadhya: “Are you free?”

Simple message. I stared at it longer than I needed to. I was free. But something inside me paused.

Me: “Yeah. Why?”

The typing dots appeared almost instantly.

Aadhya: “Nothing. Just wanted to talk.”

I could’ve called her. Instead, I typed.

Me: “About?”

A small delay this time.

Aadhya: “Nothing important.”

The irony wasn’t lost on me. I used that line often. Now she was using it.

Later that night, she called anyway. Her voice sounded normal — too normal. We spoke about classes, about a professor who gave surprise tests, about how this semester felt heavier than the last. But somewhere between sentences, there were pauses. Not comfortable ones. Measured ones.

At one point she asked quietly, “Are you okay?”

I didn’t hesitate. “Yeah. Why?”

“You sound… distant.”

I looked at the ceiling fan spinning slowly above me. “I’m just tired.”

Silence.

Then softly — “Okay.”

That word felt heavier than it should have.

The truth was, I had started thinking too much. About what this was. About where it was going. About whether I was reading into things that weren’t meant to be read. And once you start questioning something that once felt natural, it stops feeling natural.

The next day in class, Bhagya sat near us again. Not deliberately — just naturally, like she had been doing lately.

“Charan, can you explain that last step? I didn’t get it,” she asked, leaning slightly toward my notebook.

I explained. She listened carefully, nodding. “Okay, that makes sense,” she said, smiling lightly.

Karti glanced between us with an expression I couldn’t read.

After class, as we walked back toward the hostel, he nudged me. “You’re confusing yourself.”

“I’m not.”

“You are.”

“With what?”

“With everything.”

I didn’t ask him to elaborate. Because I knew what he meant.

That evening, Aadhya’s replies were slower. Shorter. Not cold — just restrained. I told myself I was imagining it. But imagination doesn’t change tone. And tone had shifted.

I wanted to ask her directly. But asking meant admitting I was affected. And I wasn’t ready to give something a name yet — not even to myself.

On Friday night, it rained. The terrace was empty for once. I stood there alone, watching water blur the campus lights into soft reflections.

My phone buzzed again.

Aadhya: “You didn’t tell me something.”

I frowned.

Me: “What?”

Aadhya: “You’ve been different.”

My chest tightened slightly.

Me: “Different how?”

A pause. Longer this time.

Aadhya: “Like you’re thinking about something you’re not telling me.”

She wasn’t wrong. But I didn’t know how to explain something I hadn’t even understood fully myself.

Me: “I’m just busy.”

The lie sat heavy.

Aadhya: “Okay.”

There it was again. That word. Short. Contained. Not angry. But not open either.

I realised something standing there in the rain. Distance doesn’t always happen because someone leaves. Sometimes it happens because someone steps back quietly.

And I had started stepping back.

Not because I cared less. But because I cared more. And that scared me.

I didn’t want to lose her. But I also didn’t want to admit how much I would miss her if she left.

So instead of moving closer… I stayed still.

And sometimes, staying still creates the biggest distance of all.
