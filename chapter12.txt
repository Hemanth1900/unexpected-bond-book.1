


The terrace was almost empty that night.

Only the distant hum of the city and the occasional bark of a stray dog reached this high. The sky wasn’t clear, but it wasn’t cloudy either — just that dull, in-between shade where stars try to exist but don’t fully show.

I had come up without thinking. Notes in hand, intention unclear.

Sometimes I came here to study.
Sometimes to escape noise.
Sometimes for no reason at all.

That night felt like the third kind.

I leaned against the low wall, flipping through pages without reading them. My mind kept drifting.

Footsteps approached behind me.

Soft. Familiar.

“You always come here when you want to think, don’t you?”

I turned.

Aadhya stood a few steps away, hair slightly messy from the wind, sleeves rolled up casually like she hadn’t planned to stay long.

“I didn’t know I had a pattern,” I said.

She smiled faintly. “You do.”

She walked closer, stopping beside the wall, not too near, not distant either.

We stood there quietly for a moment.

The kind of silence that doesn’t demand conversation.

“So… studying?” she asked, glancing at the notebook in my hand.

“Trying to.”

“Trying is different from doing.”

I laughed softly. “You sound like Karti.”

“Please. Don’t compare me to him.”

“Unfair. He’ll be hurt.”

“He survives rejection daily,” she said calmly.

That made me laugh properly.

It felt… easy.

Too easy.




The wind picked up slightly.

She tucked her hair behind her ear, looking out at the dark campus below.

“Do you ever feel like everything moves too fast?” she asked suddenly.

“All the time.”

“No… I mean life,” she clarified. “One semester ends, another begins. New people. New routines. And suddenly, things that felt normal become memories.”

I thought about that.

“Yeah,” I said. “And sometimes you don’t even realise when something becomes important until it already is.”

She looked at me then.

Not long. Not intense.

Just enough to register the words.

“Exactly,” she said quietly.




We didn’t speak for a minute after that.

The air felt heavier, but not uncomfortable.

Just aware.

She leaned slightly forward on the wall, resting her arms, staring at the faint glow of streetlights.

“Do you overthink a lot?” she asked.

“Lately? Yes.”

“About what?”

I shrugged. “Future. Choices. People. Everything.”

“People?” she repeated.

I nodded.

She didn’t ask more.

But she didn’t look away either.




“Charan.”

“Hmm?”

“You’re different these days.”

I exhaled slowly. “Different how?”

“You pause more,” she said. “Earlier, you used to just say things. Now you think before replying.”

“That’s called maturity.”

“That’s called hiding,” she corrected calmly.

I looked at her.

She held the gaze this time.

Not challenging. Not accusing.

Just… observant.

“You notice too much,” I said.

“I notice what matters.”

The sentence landed somewhere deeper than I expected.




We moved away from the wall and sat on the low steps nearby.

Closer now.

Still not touching.

The terrace light flickered once before stabilising.

“You know what’s strange?” she said.

“What?”

“Some people feel familiar even if you’ve known them only a short time.”

I didn’t respond.

“Like you don’t have to try hard around them,” she continued. “You just exist. And it feels enough.”

My chest tightened slightly.

“Yeah,” I said quietly.

She looked down at her hands, fingers loosely intertwined.

“For me… that’s rare.”

The vulnerability in her voice wasn’t loud.

But it was real.




“You don’t open up easily,” I said.

She shook her head. “Not really.”

“Then why now?”

She thought for a second before answering.

“I don’t feel judged around you.”

The words stayed suspended between us.

Heavy.

Soft.

Dangerous.




I didn’t know what to say after that.

Because anything honest would reveal too much.

And anything casual would feel like a lie.

So I stayed silent.

And she didn’t push.




The wind grew colder.

She wrapped her arms around herself slightly.

“You should go,” I said. “It’s getting late.”

“You too.”

But neither of us stood.

Not immediately.

We just sat there a little longer, as if leaving would break something unnamed.




“Charan?”

“Hmm?”

“Have you ever been scared of something good?” she asked.

I frowned. “What do you mean?”

“Like… when something feels right,” she said slowly, choosing words carefully. “And you don’t know how long it’ll stay that way.”

I didn’t answer immediately.

Because I knew exactly what she meant.

“Yes,” I said finally.

“All the time.”

She nodded, like she expected that answer.

Then stood up.

“I should go now.”

I stood too.

We walked toward the stairs together.

At the entrance, she paused.

“Goodnight,” she said.

“Goodnight.”

She turned and walked down.

I stayed there for a moment longer, watching until she disappeared.




Back on the terrace, the silence felt different.

Full.

Not empty.

That conversation hadn’t changed anything outwardly.

We were still the same people.

Same college. Same routine. Same distance.

But inside…

Something had moved closer.

Not love.

Not confession.

Just…

recognition.

And sometimes, that’s where everything begins.
