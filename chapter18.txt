
The day after the festival felt strangely quiet.

Not because the campus was silent — it never was. People still rushed to classes, bikes still screeched near the gate, someone was still arguing loudly in the corridor about attendance.

Everything was normal.

And yet… something had shifted.

Not outside.

Inside.



I saw her in the corridor before class.

Same routine. Same place. Same bag slung over one shoulder. Hair tied back again. Back to the familiar version of her that belonged to daily life.

But my mind still held the image from last night.

Lights. Colours. Her walking toward me.

It lingered longer than it should have.

She noticed me looking.

“Why are you staring like you’ve never seen me before?” she asked.

“I have,” I said. “Just… not like yesterday.”

She gave a small, knowing smile.

“Yesterday wasn’t real life.”

“Maybe that’s why it felt important.”

She didn’t respond immediately.

Just nodded once, then we walked into class.



The lecture passed in fragments.

Notes on the board. Someone asking doubts. Fans whirring overhead. Pages turning.

I was present, but not fully.

My thoughts kept drifting back to moments.

Her laugh near the food stalls.

The way she paused before answering.

The way silence between us felt intentional, not empty.

It wasn’t dramatic.

Just… deeper.

And that depth stayed.



Karti noticed.

Of course he did.

We were sitting on the hostel steps that evening when he suddenly asked,

“You and her fought?”

“No.”

“Then why are you thinking so much?”

“I always think.”

“Not like this.”

I didn’t respond.

He leaned back, watching me carefully.

“You’re scared,” he said.

“Of what?”

“Of this becoming something.”

I looked at him.

“What ‘something’?”

He smirked. “See? That reaction. Exactly that.”

I exhaled slowly.

“Nothing happened,” I said.

“Yeah,” he replied. “That’s why you’re worried.”

I frowned.

He shrugged. “Because when nothing happens but everything feels different… that’s when it gets real.”

He stood up before I could reply.

“Don’t overthink too much,” he added casually. “You’ll ruin it before it even begins.”

And just like that, he walked away.

Leaving me with words I didn’t want to examine.



That night, I waited for her message.

Not consciously.

But I noticed the waiting.

Every time my phone lit up, I checked.

Every time it wasn’t her, I put it down.

I hated that awareness.

Because it meant something had changed.

And I couldn’t pretend it hadn’t.

Finally, her message came.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Reached hostel?”

<strong>Me:</strong> “Yeah.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Good.”

A pause.

Then another message.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Yesterday was nice.”

I stared at the screen for a long second before replying.

<strong>Me:</strong> “Yeah. It was.”

Typing…

Stopping…

Typing again…

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Felt different.”

I smiled slightly.

<strong>Me:</strong> “I know.”



The conversation drifted after that.

Assignments. Random jokes. Complaints about the next lab.

But underneath it, something else moved.

Unspoken.

Present.

Growing.



A few days passed like that.

Routine resumed.

But now I noticed the spaces between things more.

Moments when she wasn’t around.

Times when conversations ended sooner than usual.

Days when she seemed distracted.

Nothing obvious.

Just subtle.

And subtle changes are the hardest to ignore.

Because you can’t point at them.

You just… feel them.



One afternoon, I saw her sitting alone near the back of the library.

Not studying.

Just staring at a page, not turning it.

I walked over quietly.

“You planning to finish that chapter by next year?” I asked.

She looked up, surprised, then smiled faintly.

“Trying to.”

“You don’t look convinced.”

“I’m just tired.”

“Of assignments?”

She hesitated.

“Of… everything moving too fast,” she said.

I sat down beside her.

“Yeah,” I replied. “Feels like we barely catch up before the next thing starts.”

She nodded slowly.

For a while, neither of us spoke.

Library silence wrapped around us.

Comfortable.

Familiar.

Then she said softly,

“Do you ever feel like… things change without asking you?”

“All the time.”

“And you don’t know whether to hold them… or let them happen?”

I turned toward her slightly.

She wasn’t looking at me.

Just staring ahead.

“I think,” I said slowly, “we try to understand them first.”

“And if we can’t?”

“Then maybe… we just stay.”

She finally looked at me.

“Stay?”

“Yeah,” I said. “Not rush. Not pull away. Just… stay where it feels right.”

Her expression softened.

Like something had eased.

“…that sounds simple,” she said.

“It isn’t,” I replied. “But it helps.”

She nodded.

And for the first time that day, she looked lighter.



Walking back from the library, I realised something quietly unsettling.

The distance between us had changed.

Not physically.

Emotionally.

We were no longer just sharing conversations.

We were sharing pauses.

Questions.

Fears we didn’t say directly.

And that closeness…

felt fragile.

Because once you cross that line, you can’t go back to being casual.



That night, I lay awake longer than usual.

Thinking.

About her.

About the way things were unfolding.

About how natural it all felt… and how easily it could break.

Because connections like this don’t shatter loudly.

They fade.

Slowly.

Quietly.

Until one day you realise something that once felt essential… isn’t there anymore.

And for the first time…

that thought scared me.

Not losing her suddenly.

But losing her gradually.

Without noticing when it began.

Without knowing how to stop it.

And maybe…

that fear itself meant something had already begun.

Something I wasn’t ready to name yet.

But couldn’t ignore anymore.
