
Nights in the hostel had a different kind of honesty.

No classrooms. No noise of routines. No pressure to appear a certain way.

Just open skies, tired minds, and conversations that slipped out without planning.

That night, I went to the terrace to clear my head.

Assignments were done. Notes were half-read. The day had been long, but not heavy.

Just… full.

The air was cooler than usual. A faint breeze moved across the open space. Someone in another block was playing music softly. Distant laughter echoed and disappeared.

I leaned against the railing, staring at the lights scattered across the campus.

That’s when my phone buzzed.

Aadhya.

Aadhya: “Awake?”
Me: “Yeah.”
Aadhya: “Where?”
Me: “Terrace.”
Aadhya: “Same.”

I turned instinctively.

And saw her.

Near the opposite side.

Phone still in hand.

She smiled when our eyes met.




“Could’ve just called,” I said as she walked closer.

“Messaging felt less dramatic.”

“Fair.”

She stood beside me, both of us facing the campus below.

Silence settled comfortably.




“Long day?” she asked.

“Hmm.”

“You look tired.”

“So do you.”

“I am.”

A pause.

Then she leaned slightly on the railing.

“Some days just feel heavier even when nothing major happens.”

I nodded.

“Like your mind is carrying more than your day.”

She looked at me, surprised.

“Exactly.”




We stayed quiet for a bit.

Wind brushing past. Someone shouting somewhere far. A dog barking in the distance.

Ordinary sounds.

But the moment felt still.




“Do you ever feel like you’re constantly catching up?” she asked.

“With?”

“Life. Expectations. People. Yourself.”

I exhaled slowly.

“All the time.”

She smiled faintly. “Good. I thought it was just me.”

“Never just you.”

The words came out before I thought about them.

She noticed.

Didn’t respond immediately.

But her expression softened.




“Back home,” she said after a moment, “everything feels simpler.”

“Because it is?”

“Because I’m not trying to be anything there. I just… exist.”

“And here?”

“Here I’m always aware. Of what I say. How I act. What people think.”

I turned slightly toward her.

“You don’t seem like that.”

“That’s because I hide it well.”

I laughed quietly. “Same.”



A breeze passed again.

Her hair shifted slightly across her face. She brushed it back absentmindedly, eyes still on the horizon.

I caught myself watching.

Not intentionally.

Just noticing.

The calm in her posture. The way she didn’t rush words. The way she allowed silence to exist without discomfort.

It felt… grounding.




“You’re very observant,” she said suddenly.

I blinked. “What?”

“You watch people a lot.”

I hesitated.

“Habit.”

“Or protection?”

That landed deeper than expected.

“Maybe both.”

She nodded slowly.

“I used to do that too,” she said. “Observe first. Speak later.”

“Used to?”

“Now I’m trying to stop overthinking everything.”

“Working?”

“Not really.”

We both laughed.




Another pause.

Longer this time.

But not awkward.

Just… shared.




“Charan,” she said softly.

“Hmm?”

“You’ve changed this semester.”

I looked at her.

“How?”

“Quieter in class. But more open otherwise.”

I didn’t realise it was visible.

“Good change or bad?”

“Different,” she said honestly. “Not bad.”

I waited.

Then she added—

“You seem more… present.”

That word stayed.




“And you?” I asked. “You’ve changed too.”

“Have I?”

“You talk more now.”

“Only with certain people.”

I understood what she meant.

Didn’t push it.




We stayed there longer than we planned.

Talking about random things.

Childhood memories. Favorite places. Annoying habits. How college felt overwhelming and exciting at the same time.

Normal conversations.

But stretched.

Layered.

Real.



At one point, she laughed — really laughed — at something I said.

Head tilted back slightly. Eyes closed for a second.

And I realised I hadn’t seen her laugh like that before.

Not fully.

It stayed with me.




“Do you ever think about the future too much?” she asked after a while.

“Yeah.”

“And get scared?”

“Sometimes.”

“Of what?”

I took a moment.

“Of losing what feels stable.”

She didn’t speak.

But she understood.

I could tell.




“I don’t plan too far,” she said quietly. “Because things change.”

“True.”

“But…” she paused, searching for words, “I like knowing some people will stay.”

That felt heavier than the sentence suggested.

I didn’t respond immediately.

Because I didn’t know how.




We both looked back at the campus.

Lights blinking. People moving. Life continuing.




After a while, she checked her phone.

“I should go. Morning class.”

“Yeah.”

She stepped back, then stopped.

“Terrace conversations are dangerous.”

I smiled faintly. “Why?”

“Because you say things you usually don’t.”

She wasn’t wrong.




As she turned to leave, she paused once more.

“Goodnight, Charan.”

“Goodnight.”

She walked toward the stairs, footsteps fading.

And suddenly, the terrace felt quieter than before.




I stayed there a little longer.

Thinking.

Not about anything specific.

Just… feeling the weight of the night.

The calm.

The connection.

The unspoken parts.

Because somewhere between conversations, laughter, and silence…

she had become more than just someone I talked to.

She had become someone my mind searched for without effort.

And that scared me more than I admitted.

Because the closer someone gets…

the harder it becomes to imagine a day without them.

And for the first time—

I didn’t want to imagine that at all.
