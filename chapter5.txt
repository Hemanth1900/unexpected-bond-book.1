

Hostel nights had a different kind of silence.

Not the peaceful kind. Not the lonely kind. Just… a steady background hum of existence. Fans spinning. Someone laughing in the corridor. A distant ringtone that never seemed to stop. Footsteps echoing and fading.

Life didn’t pause here. It just slowed down.

I was sitting on my bed, phone in hand, not doing anything important. Just scrolling through things I wasn’t really reading. My roommate had already fallen asleep, and the tube light above flickered occasionally like it couldn’t decide whether to stay awake or rest.

And then her message appeared.

No buildup. No special timing.

Just:

“Done for the day?”

I stared at it for a second before replying.

“Almost.”

There was nothing extraordinary about the conversation that followed. No emotional topic. No deep confession. Just small, scattered pieces of our day. She spoke about a class that dragged longer than expected. I mentioned nothing in particular. The kind of exchange that usually disappears the moment it ends.

But this one stayed.

Not because of what was said.

Because of how it felt.

The room felt quieter. The fan’s sound faded somewhere behind my attention. Every time the typing dots appeared, my focus shifted entirely to the screen without me realising it.

And that’s when Karti noticed.

He had been lying on the opposite bed, pretending to watch something on his phone. But Karti never really missed anything happening around him.

He sat up suddenly.

“Oi…”

I didn’t look up.

“Hmm.”

“Who?”

That was it. Straight to the point.

“No one.”

He laughed.

“Phone la yaaru illa nu sonna, adhu dhaan problem.”

I tried ignoring him.

He got off his bed and walked closer, peeking over my shoulder before I could tilt the phone away.

“Secret ah?”

“Dei… go sleep.”

“Athu later. First answer.”

Karti always spoke like everything was entertainment. Like life itself existed for commentary.

I locked my phone and placed it beside me.

He raised an eyebrow.

“Apo definitely someone.”

“Nothing like that.”

“Hmm.”

He dragged a chair and sat facing me, leaning forward like an investigator.

“You’ve been smiling at your phone a lot these days.”

I didn’t respond.

He continued.

“Night la late-ah wake. Morning la half-sleep face. Class la attention illa. Something is happening.”

“It’s nothing, da.”

“Seri.”

He leaned back, hands behind his head.

“Name enna?”

I didn’t even realise I had smiled until he pointed it out.

“There’s no name.”

“Apdiya?” he smirked. “Then why are you checking your phone every two minutes?”

I picked the phone up again, more to avoid the conversation than to reply.

Her message had come.

“You disappeared.”

I replied quickly.

“Room la disturbance.”

There was a pause.

Then:

“Best friend?”

I stared at the screen.

“Yeah.”

“Lucky.”

That word stayed longer than the rest.

Before I could think about it, I typed:

“He thinks I’m hiding something.”

“Are you?”

The question felt light. But it landed somewhere deeper.

I didn’t reply immediately.

Karti was still watching me.

“Seri, I won’t ask,” he said suddenly, standing up. “But I know one thing.”

“What?”

“You’re not the same as before.”

That caught me off guard.

He wasn’t teasing now.

His tone had shifted. Just slightly.

“Before, you didn’t care about anything. Phone la yaaru message pannaalum, reply later. Now… instant.”

I looked away.

He continued, softer.

“Something changed. That’s all.”

Then he left the room without waiting for a response.

The door closed quietly behind him.

And for the first time that night, I noticed how still everything felt.

I looked back at the phone.

Her message was still there.

“Are you?”

I typed.

“I don’t know.”

The typing dots appeared quickly this time.

“Fair.”

No judgement. No follow-up. Just acceptance.

I leaned back against the wall, holding the phone loosely.

We continued talking.

About nothing.

About everything.

About how tiring classes felt. How random hostel noises never stopped. How nights always seemed longer when there was no one around.

But with her, nights didn’t feel empty.

They felt… shared.

At one point, I realised I was describing something small from my day — something I wouldn’t normally tell anyone. Just a passing moment. A random observation.

And she listened.

Responded.

Not dramatically.

Just enough to let me know she was there.

Time moved without announcing itself.

Minutes stretched. The conversation flowed without effort. There was no pressure to keep it alive, yet neither of us let it end.

And somewhere in between those ordinary messages, something settled quietly.

Comfort.

Not loud.

Not intense.

Just steady.

For a second, I hesitated before typing her name.

It felt strange.

Until now, I hadn’t said it directly.

But it slipped out naturally.

“Aadhu… do you ever feel like days just repeat?”

The message sent before I could rethink it.

There was a small pause.

Then:

“Sometimes.”

Another message followed.

“But repetition isn’t always bad.”

I read it again.

“Why?”

“Because some people become part of that repetition.”

I didn’t reply.

Not immediately.

Because I didn’t know if she realised what she had just said.

Or what it meant to me.

Karti returned after a while, holding two cups of tea.

He placed one beside me.

“Take.”

I nodded.

He sat down on the floor this time, back resting against the bed.

“Still talking?”

I didn’t answer.

He smirked.

“Seri. I’ll stop.”

We sat in silence for a while.

Fan spinning. Distant laughter. The taste of hot tea slowly fading.

And in the middle of all that ordinary life…

I realised something.

This wasn’t just inside my head anymore.

It had entered my routine.

My room.
My nights.
My friendships.

Karti had noticed. The hostel atmosphere had noticed. Even I couldn’t ignore it now.

This wasn’t just a presence that lived in thoughts.

It was beginning to exist in real life.

And for the first time…

that felt both comforting and terrifying at the same time.

Because once something becomes part of your real world…

losing it no longer feels distant.

It feels possible.
