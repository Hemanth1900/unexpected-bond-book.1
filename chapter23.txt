

Some moments look ordinary while they’re happening.

Later… you realise they shifted something quietly.

That day began like any other.

Assignments overdue.
Clouds gathering since morning.
That heavy, metallic smell in the air that announces rain before it arrives.

By afternoon, the sky gave in.

Classes ended early because half the electricity went out. Students rushed toward the bus stop outside campus, trying to avoid getting drenched.

I didn’t.

I walked slowly.

Because she was walking beside me.



The rain started as a drizzle.

Then turned impatient.

Within minutes, the road was shining silver. Water gathered along the edges. Buses were delayed.

We stood under the small tin shelter near the stop.

Too many people. Too little space.

She stood close.

Closer than usual.

Drops of water clung to the ends of her hair. Her sleeves were slightly damp. She kept brushing strands away from her face, failing every time.

“You didn’t bring an umbrella?” she asked.

“I don’t check weather apps.”

“That’s not confidence. That’s stupidity.”

“Same thing.”

She laughed.

The sound mixed with rain.

For a few seconds, the world shrank to just that bus stop.



Thunder cracked somewhere in the distance.

She instinctively stepped half a step closer.

Not dramatic.

Just reflex.

I noticed.

Didn’t move away.



<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You like rain?”

“Yeah.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Why?”

I thought about it.

“Because everything slows down.”

She tilted her head slightly.

“Slows down?”

“Yeah. People rush less. Noise feels softer. Even conversations feel… different.”

She looked at the road for a moment.

Then said quietly,

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “I like it because it hides things.”

I frowned. “Hides?”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “You can cry in rain and no one will know.”

That sentence landed unexpectedly.

I turned toward her.

She wasn’t smiling.

Just watching the rain.

And for the first time in a while, I felt like there was more beneath her calm than she showed.



“Do you cry often?” I asked carefully.

She gave me a side glance.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Do you?”

Touché.

I exhaled lightly.

“No.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Neither do I.”

But her tone didn’t fully agree with her words.



The crowd shifted as a bus approached.

Not ours.

People moved forward.

We stayed back.

Rain intensified, bouncing off the road in tiny splashes.

She wrapped her arms loosely around herself, slightly cold.

Without thinking, I said, “You’ll get sick.”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “From rain?”

“From not taking care.”

She looked at me for a second.

Not teasing.

Not joking.

Just… noticing.



Another bus passed.

Still not ours.

The shelter emptied a little.

Now we stood almost alone.

The rain was louder.

Closer.

The kind that forces you to speak softer because it fills the space around you.



<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Do you ever think we’re… too comfortable?”

I blinked.

“With what?”

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “With this.”

She gestured between us vaguely.

The air shifted.

Rain continued, indifferent.

“I don’t think comfort is bad,” I said.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “It isn’t.”

Pause.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “But comfort sometimes makes you forget what something actually means.”

I didn’t answer immediately.

Because I understood what she was hinting at.

We had never defined anything.

Never named it.

Just… existed inside it.

And maybe that was both safe and dangerous.



Thunder rolled again.

This time closer.

She looked at me suddenly.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “If one day this changes… will you tell me?”

The question was direct.

Honest.

No shield around it.

“Yes,” I said immediately.

No hesitation.

Because that answer felt instinctive.

She nodded slightly.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “Good.”



Our bus finally appeared in the distance.

Headlights cutting through rain.

People began gathering again.

The moment started dissolving into noise.

But before stepping forward, she said one more thing.

Soft enough that I almost missed it.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “I don’t like not knowing where I stand.”

That sentence stayed with me longer than the rain did.



We boarded.

Crowded.

Standing close because there was no space.

The bus jerked forward.

She grabbed the handle above.

Our shoulders touched.

Neither of us moved away.

Rain blurred the windows.

City lights stretched into streaks.

Everything outside looked distorted.

But inside…

everything felt clearer than before.



I realised something during that ride.

We weren’t drifting anymore.

We weren’t stepping back.

But we were approaching a point where silence wouldn’t be enough.

Comfort was growing.

Attachment was growing.

And sooner or later…

it would demand a name.

As the bus slowed near her stop, she looked at me.

No dramatic smile.

No long stare.

Just steady eye contact.

<strong>Aadhya:</strong> “See you tomorrow?”

“Yeah.”

She stepped off into the rain again.

Didn’t run.

Just walked.

And I watched until she disappeared behind moving vehicles.

That rain didn’t confess anything.

Didn’t change our status.

Didn’t declare love.

But it shifted something quiet inside me.

Because when she said she didn’t like not knowing where she stood…

I realised I didn’t either.

And maybe…

the next time the sky decided to pour like that…

one of us would finally say what we had both been circling around for months.

And that possibility…

felt closer than ever.
